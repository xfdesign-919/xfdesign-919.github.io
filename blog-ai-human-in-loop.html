Why AI in Cybersecurity Needs a Human in the Loop

July 2026 · 5 min read

Every SOC intern this summer is hearing the same message: AI tools are changing how security teams monitor, triage, and respond to threats. What most of us aren't told clearly enough is exactly how much to trust them.

What AI Gets Right

This summer, I've used AI-assisted tooling to help sort through detections and speed up documentation - and it does genuinely help. It also gets things wrong with complete confidence, which is a much larger problem in security than it might sound.

IBM has written specifically about this risk, describing how AI hallucinations, confident but incorrect outputs, pose a real cybersecurity concern, and not just an inconvenience. In a field where a missed or misclassified detection can mean a real breach, an AI tool sounding certain can't be treated the same as being correct.

Where Judgment Still Matters

The lesson I've taken from this isn't to avoid AI. Instead, it's to treat these tool's output as a lead instead of a verdict. I check severity classifications against the raw data. I verify anything an AI-assisted tool flags before I escalate it. That extra step costs just a few minutes, skipping it can cost a headache-inducing-amount more.

I think this is the actual skill security teams need going forward: not AI literacy in the abstract, but the specific habit of verifying before trusting. These tools are only as good as the judgment sitting on top of them, and that judgment is still very much a human job.
