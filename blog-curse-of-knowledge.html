The Curse of Knowledge in Technical Communication

July 2026 · 5 min read

A few weeks into my internship, I wrote up a security detection I was genuinely proud of. It was more than thorough, it was well-polished, and it was strong. It was also, as I'd soon learn, almost useless to the person who actually needed it.

Why Experts Struggle to Explain

I handed it to someone on the service desk who needed to know just two things: was this real, and what should they do about it? Instead, I'd given them a wall of technical classifications. They read it, paused, and asked me the exact question my report was supposed to answer.

This is a well documented phenomenon called the curse of knowledge, and researchers have described it as a cognitive bias where the memory of how we learned something gets inflated, making it genuinely difficult to recall what it felt like not to know it. Once you understand something deeply, communicating it in simple terms can become shockingly hard. This doesn't mean it's because you're a bad communicator, but rather that your brain has stopped tracking which parts require explanation.

How I'm Learning to Bridge the Gap

I've started treating this as an actual design constraint on my writing rather than a soft skill to improve someday. Before sending a report, I like to ask: could someone outside this specialty read this and know exactly what to do next? If the answer is no, I haven't finished writing it yet - no matter how technically accurate it is.

The uncomfortable truth is that being right isn't the same as being useful. In security especially, a correct finding nobody can act on might as well not exist.
